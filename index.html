Lepidoptera · Spain & Europe
Marsh
Fritillary
Euphydryas aurinia
EN — Endangered

One of Europe's most threatened butterflies. Its caterpillars feed exclusively on Devil's-bit Scabious — a plant vanishing with the meadows that host it. Once common across Spain, colonies are now fragmented and isolated.

02 / 05
Coleoptera · Alpine forests
Alpine
Longhorn
Rosalia alpina
VU — Vulnerable

A jewel of old-growth beech forests, its iridescent blue-grey wings make it one of Europe's most beautiful beetles. Threatened by logging and the removal of dead wood — the very habitat its larvae need to survive.

03 / 05
Hymenoptera · Northern Europe
Short-haired
Bumblebee
Bombus subterraneus
CR — Critically Endangered

Once widespread across Europe, this bumblebee has collapsed to near-extinction. The loss of wildflower meadows, pesticide use and climate change have made it one of the rarest pollinators on the continent. Every colony counts.

04 / 05
Lepidoptera · Mediterranean
Lappet
Moth
Gastropacha quercifolia
VU — Vulnerable

Camouflaged as a dead leaf, this remarkable moth is a master of disguise — but no disguise protects against habitat destruction. Orchards and hedgerows where its caterpillars feed are disappearing across the Iberian Peninsula.

Lepidoptera · Spain endemic
Spanish
Fritillary
Euphydryas desfontainii
EN — Endangered

Endemic to the Iberian Peninsula and North Africa, this fritillary is one of the species Ana's foundation actively monitors. It lives only in specific dry scrubland communities — habitats shrinking under land-use change and drought.

Founder

Ana
Jurat
García

For five years I've witnessed firsthand how fragile the insect world in Europe really is — and how fast we can lose what we take for granted.

In my native Spain, the decline of insect species became impossible to ignore. So I built a foundation that does something about it: funding research, supporting conservation projects, and connecting the people who act.

Growing up, my father — Prof. Juan Luis Jurat-Fuentes, professor of entomology — taught me to look at insects with curiosity, not fear. He handed me a magnifying glass before I could read. That perspective shaped everything.

What began as a family passion became an urgent mission. If we do nothing, future generations will never see some of these species. That is not acceptable.

Prof. Juan Luis Jurat-Fuentes
Professor of Entomology — the scientist who taught a daughter that every insect has a story worth protecting.

Our Work

Projects
that count.

01

EMBRACE / eBMS

We joined the pan-European EMBRACE project, reinforcing the butterfly monitoring network eBMS and updating meadow ecosystem health indicators. Butterflies are the most visible signal of ecosystem collapse — when they vanish, the chain breaks.

2025 – 2026
02

LIFE Crau Grasshopper

We contributed to the LIFE SOS programme saving Prionotropis rhodanica — a critically endangered grasshopper endemic to the Crau steppe. Habitat restoration, population monitoring, reintroduction. Methods that work in one region save species in another.

2021 – 2026
03

Crisis Response

During the Cumbre Vieja eruption on La Palma (2021), beekeepers rescued hives from the lava zone. Thousands of native black bees survived 50+ days under volcanic ash. We documented this response model as proof that life can be protected even in catastrophe.

Why It Matters

Not a
"green"
topic.

This is about food on our table. About the stability of agriculture. About the health of every ecosystem that sustains human life.

Protecting insects is not a niche interest — it is a civilisational obligation. The foundation supports only projects with measurable results: from monitoring to direct action on the ground.

"Endangered insects don't respect borders. Methods refined in one region can save a species in another — that's the logic we operate by."
— Ana Jurat García, Founder
